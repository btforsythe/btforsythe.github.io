On a tangential note, it also turns out that public libraries let just about anyone in and let him borrow books. In my browsing, I discovered Edward Hirsch's How to Read a Poem: And Fall in Love with Poetry. I haven't read enough of it to give an opinion. What I can give is a quotation I stumbled over while flipping through that floored me (or would have, had I not been conveniently sitting on the floor in the stacks).

I've been struggling with the nature of a poem, the purpose of a poem, the end of a poem, so this struck close to home. I hadn't even heard of Paul Valéry until I ran across this. (There go my literary credentials!) I take it from my exhaustive research (I googled it) that this particular quote was (translated?) from a lecture he gave in 1939 at Oxford. Were he a hack, though, this would be none the worse for it:

"A poet's function—do not be startled by this remark—is not to experience the poetic state: that is a private affair. His function is to create it in others."

Many parts of this book were originally published in magazine columns. Poetry Foundation has reprinted the first chapter from the book. They even broke it down into easy-to-consume-via-browser sections.
